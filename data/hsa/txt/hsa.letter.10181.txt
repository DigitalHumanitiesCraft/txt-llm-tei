Kertch, August 1. 1895.
Dear Sir,
I am much obliged to you for your kind letter of the 11th ult.° My Georgian studies have not by any means progressed so far as you seem to think. In 1887 I wrote a little book of travel called „The Kingdom of Georgia“ (note 1), which is now out of print, but I have given orders that a bound copy of the rough proofs should be sent to you from England, if this can be found. Last year I published in Boston a translation of Professor Tsagareli’s  Catalogue of the Georgian MSS. in Jerusalem: I send you a copy. (note 2) I also published a translation of Saba Orbeliani’s „Book of Wisdom and Lies“ (წიგნი სიბრძნე სიცრუისა), published in an editon de luxe by Mr. William Morris, but I hear that it is exhausted. (note 3) My sister has translated a collection of Georgian „Folk Tales“ (publ.
d by David Nutt) (note 4), and she is now seeking to print a verse translation of Dchavdchavadze’s „Hermit“ (note 5) (
განდეგილი) &amp; other poems.
My friend C. Dodashvili printed in მანდემილი an intresting grammatical sketch (note 6) which I had completely translated. Unfortunately, my ms. seems to have been lost or stolen during my journey to the Crimea. I should recommend you to write to Dodashvili  for information about grammatical questions. His address is:
Тифлись
Kbaшbemckaя цepкobь
Koncmaнmuнy Дeoдaшbuam. (note 7)
It is difficult to get the older books you mention, but if you apply to the Tpybuнckoe Издameлъckoe Тobapщecmbo in Tiflis I think they could send you the grammars of Cliphiani (note 8), Antoni (note 9), Zordania (note 10) and the 1886 edition of Chubinov’s dictionary (Georgian-Russian &amp; Russ. Georgian) (note 11) which is much better than the 1840 bilingual lexicon. (note 12) Though is has still many faults.

Tsagareli (note 13) was here with us in Kertch about a fortnight ago, and stayed three days, on his way to Kislovodsk. (note 14) He, like all his Countrymen, rejoices to see that you are seriously occupying yourself with the language.
The lack of intelligible grammars and philological works on Georgian is the chief cause why so little is known of the language and literature in Europe. The beginner has to depend almost entirely on Brosset, who is nearly worthless. (note 15)
I look forward with pleasure to the receipt of your works

Beleave me, dear Sir,

Very Sincerely Yours,

Oliver Wardrop
